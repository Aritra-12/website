Helly's Theorem – (Eduard Helly, 1913)

Helly's theorem is a basic result in discrete geometry on the intersection of convex sets. Let $X_1,X_2,...,X_n$ be a finite collection of convex subsets of $\mathbb{R}^d$, with $n>d+1$. Helly's theorem states the following: \[\text{If }\forall\{i_1,i_2,...,i_{d+1}\}\in\{1,2,...,n\}, \ \bigcap_{j=1}^{d+1}X_{i_j}\neq\emptyset\text{ then } \bigcap_{j=1}^{n}X_j\neq\emptyset.\]In simpler terms, if the intersection of any $d+1$ sets among $X_1,X_2,...,X_n$ is nonempty, then so is the intersection of all of the $n$ sets.

Helly's theorem is very volatile and there are several extensions to it. Two great articles which prove and study this result more thoroughly are:

  • Helly’s Theorem: New Variations and Applications – Nina Amenta, Jesús A. De Loera, and Pablo Soberón (arXiv:1508.07606v2 [math.MG] 8 Mar 2016)
  • Helly's Theorem and its Equivalences via Convex Analysis – Adam Robinson
